# Mapping Arlan Rakhmetzhanov's Influences and Worldview

## Foundational Family Influence

**Sanzhar Rakhmetzhanov (Father)** stands as Arlan's earliest and most formative influence. During childhood walks along a river in Almaty, Kazakhstan, his father introduced him to the concept of Y Combinator:

> "Arlan, there are several types of universities: some where people go just to get a degree, and others that are designed specifically for founders. There's something called Y Combinator, where founders pitch their ideas and, if accepted, receive funding and access to an incredible network to help build their companies... One day, I want you to get in. It's in the heart of Silicon Valley—where dreams are built." [Source 5]

This conversation proved prophetic. Years later, Arlan notes: **"A couple of years ago, my dad actually applied to YC with one of his startups, but it ultimately didn't get in. It made one thing clear to me: I have to finish the work."** [Source 5] This created a generational mission that drove Arlan through two rejections before his acceptance to YC S25.

His parents **Valeria and Sanzhar** together "helped shape this passion early on" for technology [Source 4]. His father, a technology entrepreneur with extensive business experience, now co-founded Armeta AI with Arlan's older brother Temirlan [Source 5].

## Silicon Valley Heroes: The Mythology That Shaped Him

### Early Obsession (Age 10-11)

**"Around the age of 10 or 11, I started reading about entrepreneurship, Silicon Valley, and Steve Jobs,"** Arlan recalls [Source 4]. His systematic study of Silicon Valley's origins included:

- **Frederick Terman** - Stanford's "father of Silicon Valley"
- **Fairchild Semiconductor** - The company that birthed the semiconductor industry
- **Steve Jobs** - The canonical entrepreneur-visionary

As he reflects: **"I began digging deeper into the roots of Silicon Valley, mostly by reading about Fairchild Semiconductor, Frederick Terman, and Steve Jobs. I became obsessed."** [Source 5]

### The Stanford Lecture Circuit (Age 16)

**"When I was 16, I watched old Stanford University lectures on startups on YouTube, including talks by Paul Graham and Sam Altman, and wondered what it would feel like to be in those rooms."** [Source 7]

These weren't casual viewings—they were formative experiences that created a specific vision for his future. Remarkably, he later gave talks at both Stanford and Columbia as a high school dropout: **"Both were dream schools of mine about 10 months ago, when I was stressed about my SAT and final exams. I never imagined that I would be speaking there, not as a student, but as a guest."** [Source 7]

## Paul Graham: The Philosophical North Star

Paul Graham's influence appears repeatedly and explicitly in Arlan's thinking. His essay **"How to Get Startup Ideas"** directly shaped Arlan's approach:

> "In one of Paul Graham's essays, How to Get Startup Ideas, I came across a quote that caught my attention: 'When searching for ideas, look in areas where you have some expertise.' It sounds simple, even obvious, yet many people overlook it." [Source 2]

Arlan applies this ruthlessly to himself:

> "I've been there myself, trying to go into fields like quantum physics or blockchain—areas where I was completely dumb. To launch a successful startup, you need at least some foundational knowledge in the domain you're tackling." [Source 2]

## The Elon Musk Self-Teaching Model

Arlan explicitly references **Elon Musk's autodidactic approach** as validation for domain expertise:

> "Elon Musk didn't just decide one day to build rockets; he spent years teaching himself aerospace engineering, destroying books on physics, rocket design, and the underlying science. Having a base level of expertise isn't just helpful—it's often a prerequisite for creating something that people want." [Source 2]

This philosophy manifests in Arlan's own journey: despite "skipping some basics" in coding initially, he's now **"taking Harvard's CS50"** to properly learn fundamentals [Source 2].

## Academic Mentors & Technical Guides

### Professor Ilya Strebulaev (Stanford)

**"I cold-emailed over 300 professors, and eventually became a research assistant to Professor Ilya Strebulaev, founder of the Venture Capital Initiative at Stanford."** [Source 4]

This wasn't random hustle—it was **systematic, focused outreach** driven by a specific goal: understanding venture capital from the inside.

### Andrej Karpathy (OpenAI)

Arlan had a pivotal technical conversation with one of AI's leading researchers:

> "A couple of months ago, I had brunch with Andrej Karpathy and asked him about continual learning for LLMs, specifically how they might prevent hallucinations when dealing with up-to-date context." [Source 3]

Karpathy's response became central to Arlan's technical obsession:

> "He pointed out that enabling models to learn continuously is still an unsolved challenge, which makes the field 10x more fascinating... As he said in a recent interview: 'These models, when you boot them up, always restart from scratch… continual learning is absent.' That really resonated with me." [Source 3]

This conversation crystallized Arlan's mission: **"I'm probably the most obsessed person in the universe when it comes to this problem, and it's exactly why I want to build Nozomio to make sure models resemble how humans learn."** [Source 3]

## Core Philosophical Frameworks

### "Move Fast and Break Things"

**"That's what differentiates startups from established big tech companies—their ability to move fast and break things."** [Source 2]

### Build Before Pitching

His failed first startup **DresserAI** taught him a brutal lesson:

> "For over three months, my team and I focused on customer development and refining our pitch deck, but all our effort led nowhere... We convinced ourselves we were making progress, but in reality, we spent countless hours brainstorming new features and imagining what the app could look like without ever actually building it." [Source 2]

**Key lesson**: "If you want to build something people want, start creating it. Test your hypotheses, develop an MVP, and avoid getting lost in endless ideas and possibilities that never materialize into real, tangible products." [Source 2]

### The Power of Momentum

> "Once you gain momentum, you can go into 'beast mode' for days or even weeks (even without a lot of expertise in the domain)... In just 3–4 months, I launched four different iOS apps and an AI SaaS product. Together, they got more than 70,000 visits and 6,000 downloads." [Source 2]
