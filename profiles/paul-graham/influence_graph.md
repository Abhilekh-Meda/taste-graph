# Mapping Paul Graham's Influences and Worldview

## People Who Shaped His Thinking

### The Explicit Heroes

In his 2008 essay "Some Heroes," Paul Graham provides the most direct window into who has shaped his worldview. His litmus test is revealing: **"When I thought about what it meant to call someone a hero, it meant I'd decide what to do by asking what they'd do in the same situation."** This is a stricter standard than mere admiration. [Source 8]

After examining this list, Graham identified a clear pattern: **"Everyone on the list had two qualities: they cared almost excessively about their work, and they were absolutely honest. By honest I don't mean trustworthy so much as that they never pander."** [Source 8]

His heroes include:

**Early Influence - Jack Lambert**: The Pittsburgh Steelers linebacker represented an escape from suburban mediocrity. Graham writes: "He seemed to regard it as a personal insult when someone from the other team had possession of the ball on his side of the line of scrimmage... He was like nothing else I'd seen." [Source 8]

**Intellectual Model - Kenneth Clark**: The art historian whom Graham calls "the best nonfiction writer I know of, on any subject." He describes reading Clark's "The Nude" as "like a ride in a Ferrari... His brain throws off ideas almost too fast to grasp them." [Source 8]

**Formative Teacher - Larry Mihalko**: His 4th grade teacher who "was intellectually curious" and "seemed like he actually wanted to be our friend." Graham notes: "When I look back it's like there's a line drawn between third and fourth grade. After Mr. Mihalko, everything was different." [Source 8]

**Technical Integrity - Robert Morris**: Graham's co-founder and collaborator has "a very unusual quality: he's never wrong... He has an almost superhuman integrity." Graham adds: "I know how hard it is, because since meeting Robert I've tried to do in software what he seems to do in hardware." [Source 7, 8]

**Literary Standard - Jane Austen**: Graham considers her "the best novelist of all time," noting that "in her novels I can't see the gears at work... her stories don't seem made up. I feel like I'm reading a description of something that actually happened." [Source 8]

### Key Collaborators and Mentors

**Jessica Livingston** (wife and Y Combinator co-founder): Described as having "x-ray vision for character," with the internal nickname "The Social Radar." Graham writes: "Everything we did as an organization went through her first — who to fund, what to say to the public, how to deal with other companies, who to hire, everything." [Source 4]

**Gino Lee**: Taught Graham a crucial test for meaningful work: **"to try to do things that would make your friends say wow."** [Source 2]

**Robert Morris and Trevor Blackwell**: Co-founders of both Viaweb and Y Combinator, described as "the two most independent-minded people I know, and in completely different ways." [Source 7]

## Intellectual Traditions and Frameworks

### The MIT/Stanford Philosophy vs. "Worse is Better"

Graham extensively engages with Richard Gabriel's concept of **"Worse is Better"** versus what Gabriel called "the right thing" (MIT approach). [Source 9]

The MIT approach prioritizes:
- **Simplicity in interface** over implementation
- **Correctness** in all observable aspects
- **Consistency** as important as correctness  
- **Completeness** covering all reasonably expected cases

The "Worse is Better" (New Jersey) approach prioritizes:
- **Simplicity in implementation** over interface
- Being simple over being correct
- Dropping edge cases rather than introducing complexity
- Sacrificing completeness for simplicity [Source 9]

Graham grappled with this tension throughout his career, even writing essays under the pseudonym "Nickieben Bourbaki" arguing against his own "Worse is Better" position. [Source 9]

### The Lisp Tradition

**John McCarthy's** invention of Lisp represents a crucial intellectual foundation. Graham writes: "Practically every programming language invented in the last 20 years includes ideas from Lisp... In 1958 these ideas were anything but obvious... McCarthy alone bridged the gap. He designed a language that was math." [Source 8]

Graham's commitment to **bottom-up programming** comes from the Lisp tradition: "In Lisp, you don't just write your program down toward the language, you also build the language up toward your program." [Source 6]

### The Essay Tradition

While **Montaigne** invented the essay form, Graham interestingly notes he failed his personal hero test. However, Graham clearly works in Montaigne's tradition, defining an essay as "something you write to try to figure something out" rather than defending a pre-chosen position. [Source 6]

## Books and Ideas Frequently Referenced

**Hardy's "A Mathematician's Apology"**: Graham specifically cites this in a footnote: "This idea I learned from Hardy's A Mathematician's Apology, which I recommend to anyone ambitious to do great work, in any field." [Source 3]

**Kenneth Clark's works**: "The Nude" and "Civilisation" - Graham recommends the latter as the single book to read about art history, calling it "much better than the drab Sears Catalogs of art that undergraduates are forced to buy for Art History 101." [Source 8]

**P.G. Wodehouse**: Represents the ideal of ignoring critics and writing exactly what you want. "Wodehouse didn't give a damn what anyone thought of him. He wrote exactly what he wanted." [Source 8]

## Schools of Thought and Philosophical Positions

### Against Traditional Philosophy

Graham concluded during his PhD work that **"AI, as practiced at the time, was a hoax"** and that traditional academic philosophy was largely "a waste of time." He argues: **"the most important principle of philosophy: that words break if you push them too far."** Most philosophical debates are "not merely afflicted by but driven by confusions over words." [Source 6]

### On Work and Productivity

Graham advocates for work that **"doesn't seem like work"** - activities you'd do even if you weren't paid. He learned this through painful experience: "That about sums up my experience of graduate school" when describing how thinking work "should hurt" led him astray. [Source 2]

### The User-Centric Design Philosophy

From his essay on design: **"The answer to the paradox, I think, is that you have to design for the user, but you have to design what the user needs, not simply what he says he wants."** [Source 2]

## Public Disagreements and Critiques

### Against Meanness

In "Mean People Fail," Graham argues: **"Being mean makes you stupid... Startups don't win by attacking. They win by transcending."** He observes that "there are next to none among the most successful people I know" who are mean. [Source 10]

### Against Trolls and Haters

Graham has written extensively about online behavior, distinguishing between criticism and trolling. He notes that **"Haters are just fanboys with the sign switched"** - both are obsessive and uncritical, just in opposite directions. [Source 10]

### The Controversy Around "Worse is Better"

Graham has publicly wrestled with his own positions, even publishing opposing essays. In the early 1990s, he wrote "Worse Is Better Is Worse" under a pseudonym attacking his own earlier position, then later wrote "Is Worse Really Better?" arguing for it again. He admits: "I still can't decide." [Source 9]

## Key Insights on His Worldview

**Honesty as Core Value**: The common thread among his heroes is that "they never pander: they never say or do something because that's what the audience wants." [Source 8]

**Empiricism Over Authority**: Graham's approach emphasizes learning from direct experience. He dropped out of art school because "I was basically teaching myself to paint, and I could do that for free." [Source 7]

**Builder's Mentality**: Nearly all his heroes made things - whether paintings (Leonardo, Calder), books (Austen, Wodehouse), programming languages (McCarthy), or companies. Even the Spitfire represents "extraordinary devotion went into it, and extraordinary courage came out." [Source 8]

**Intellectual Honesty**: From Robert Morris, he learned the value of being "correct about how correct he is" - carefully qualifying claims rather than overstating. [Source 8]

---

**The through-line in Graham's influences** is a consistent emphasis on intellectual honesty, making things rather than just talking about them, working on what genuinely interests you, and maintaining integrity even when it's unpopular. His heroes span football players, teachers, artists, writers, and technologists - but all share an excessive care for their work and a refusal to pander to audiences.
