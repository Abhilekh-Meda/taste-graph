TasteGraph: An Engine for Reconstructing How Experts Actually Think

---

WHAT WE BUILT

TasteGraph is a system that reconstructs the full topology of how any public expert thinks — not just their stated opinions, but the actual pattern of their taste — and uses it to judge anything you submit to it. You give it a person's name and something to evaluate. It autonomously researches that person across the internet, builds a multi-dimensional taste profile with seven distinct analytical layers, and produces a structured verdict that reads like it came directly from that person's mind — grounded in their documented words, their documented contradictions, and their documented errors.

The core insight is that taste is pattern recognition, not opinion. Most people's stated values diverge systematically from their actual reactions. A system that only reads someone's essays will produce verdicts that are inconsistent with how they actually respond to things in the real world. TasteGraph is built around closing that gap. It is not a chatbot that writes in someone's style. It is an evaluation engine that models someone's judgment, including the parts of their judgment they would not be able to articulate themselves.

THE PROBLEM WE ARE SOLVING

There are many AI tools that will summarize what a person has said publicly or generate text "in their style." None of them do what TasteGraph does: model the full complexity of how someone evaluates things, including where their stated principles contradict their documented behavior, how their taste has shifted over time, what categories they reliably misjudge, who they were influenced by, and how their linguistic patterns signal genuine excitement versus polite approval.

We care about this because the people whose taste matters most — the early investors, the editors, the builders who have been right about the future — have developed judgment that is genuinely hard to access. They are few, they are busy, they evaluate selectively, and their feedback is not legible to most founders and creators. Getting honest, substantive feedback from Paul Graham is almost impossible. Getting feedback that reflects how he actually judges things — not just what he says he values — is completely impossible by any other means. TasteGraph makes that judgment accessible and decomposable.

We also care about it because of a specific problem with LLMs. Large language models have taste — implicit aesthetic preferences that shape what they generate — but that taste is usually invisible and unjustifiable. Training a model on an expert's full documented reaction history, including where they contradict themselves, changes the quality of evaluation it can produce. TasteGraph is a bet that encoding the full topology of taste, not just stated values, is a research contribution, not just a product feature.

THE SEVEN LAYERS OF TASTE WE MODEL

1. STATED VS. ACTUAL (The Contradiction Layer)

Real experts contradict themselves. Their essays claim one thing; their documented reactions reveal another. We index both separately and model the gap as a first-class output. Every verdict includes a contradiction callout that surfaces where the person's stated values conflict with their actual historical reactions, and how that tension shifts the specific verdict being rendered.

For Paul Graham, this gap is wide and well-documented. He explicitly champions meritocracy in his essays: "Focus on the user," "Make something people want," "The way we got so many female founders was by being less biased." But his actual funding decisions weight culturally-specific founder traits heavily — communication style, accent clarity, fit with Silicon Valley conversational norms. He rejected founders for accents "so strong that people can't understand them" while claiming pure meritocracy. He praised Airbnb primarily for a cereal box story demonstrating "resourcefulness and determination" — a culturally-specific American entrepreneurial narrative — while claiming to evaluate ideas on merit. He admitted: "I actually have bad judgment of character. I'm not good at judging people. But Jessica — she is very rarely wrong." His stated framework is "make something people want." His actual framework is "be the kind of person who fits the Silicon Valley founder archetype; then we'll help you figure out what to make."

TasteGraph indexes both sides and makes the tension explicit in every verdict. The contradiction callout is a dedicated visual element on the page, not a parenthetical.

2. TASTE DRIFT (The Temporal Layer)

People's taste changes over time. Early Paul Graham cared intensely about programming language purity — he founded Viaweb in Lisp and wrote extensively about the competitive advantage of language choice. He made confident predictions about Java's failure ("No one loves it. It's designed for large organizations"). He wrote "Microsoft Is Dead" in 2007.

Later Paul Graham cares much more about founder determination and product sense. His 2020s writing is dominated by clarity, simplicity, and the character of people he wants to fund. The Lisp essays feel like a different person.

A flat snapshot of someone's views — averaging across everything they have ever said — produces a verdict that is true of no particular moment and useful for no particular decision. TasteGraph weights recent signals more heavily than older ones and surfaces the timeline of drift explicitly. The verdict you receive is time-aware. The methodology section shows you the weighting: "Signals from 2015–present weighted at 1.8x relative to pre-2010 signals." You are not getting an average. You are getting the person as they are now, informed by who they were.

3. BLIND SPOTS (The Calibration Layer)

Every expert has categories of things they systematically misjudge. Paul Graham told Reddit's founders that their company name was "terrible" and would be "poison to potential investors." He suggested they abandon the Snoo alien mascot and offered alternatives including "Octopop." Reddit is now worth $38 billion. The alien is tattooed on people's bodies. Graham's consumer brand judgment is documented as systematically unreliable.

He also described the startup rate as rising with inequality in the years since 1980 — a factual error. The startup rate has actually declined as inequality has risen. His Silicon Valley vantage point distorted his macroeconomic analysis.

TasteGraph models these documented error categories and includes a dedicated blind spot warning card in every verdict. The card tells you what category the submission falls into, whether that overlaps with documented errors, and what correction to apply. The score you see already incorporates this correction — it is not a disclaimer, it is a calculation.

4. INFLUENCE GRAPH (The Lineage Layer)

Taste is social. Paul Graham's aesthetic was shaped by specific people and intellectual traditions: the Lisp hacker culture of MIT and ITA Software, Robert Morris as a technical benchmark, Trevor Blackwell as a model of hacker-engineer excellence, Jessica Livingston as his "social radar" for reading people. His entrepreneurial worldview came from running Viaweb and being acquired by Yahoo — a specific set of experiences that shaped what he thinks "building a company" means.

When you judge something through Paul Graham, you are not judging it through a single person. You are running it through his whole intellectual lineage. The influence lineage note in the methodology section surfaces which parts of that lineage are most activated by your specific submission. If you are submitting something technical, the Lisp-era hacker aesthetic gets weighted. If you are submitting something about founders and character, the post-2010 YC model gets weighted. This affects the output, not just the display.

5. CONTEXTUAL VARIATION (The Stakes Layer)

The same person judges differently depending on the stakes and framing. Paul Graham at a hackathon is explicitly lowering his bar: novelty and ambition matter more than defensibility. Paul Graham making a YC acceptance decision is weighing whether these are the people he wants to spend the next decade rooting for. Paul Graham writing an essay is in a different mode entirely — essayistic exploration, not investment judgment.

TasteGraph makes context a first-class input. Before you submit, you specify the judging context. The verdict renders for that context, and the context variance section shows you the alternative scores: how the verdict would shift if the context were different, and specifically what changes and why. The diff is shown explicitly. "As a Series A investor, PG would weight defensibility and market structure more heavily; this submission does not address either, which pulls the score down by 0.5." You can see the lever being pulled.

6. LINGUISTIC TELLS (The Confidence Layer)

Experts signal genuine approval and disapproval in specific linguistic patterns that are detectable at scale across a large enough corpus. Paul Graham's tells are well-characterized because he has published extensively and his language is distinctive.

His excitement markers: "the most exciting kind of idea" — reserved for ideas that seem both preposterous and true. "To my surprise" — genuine discovery. "Wonderfully" — aesthetic appreciation. He reserves "excited" specifically for deep intellectual engagement; he distinguishes it explicitly from "happy." He rarely uses "amazing" or "brilliant" — those are weak signals in his corpus because they are too common.

His uncertainty markers saturate his essays: "I suspect," "probably," "my guess is," "it seems," "arguably," "perhaps." He stacks them. This extensive hedging is not weakness — it is epistemic rigor. But it means that when he drops the hedges, it signals high confidence. An unhedged claim from Paul Graham is information.

His disapproval markers are rare and therefore strong: "terrible mistake," "one of the worst," "appallingly," "waste of time." He uses them precisely because he deploys them so infrequently.

TasteGraph indexes these patterns across his full published corpus and uses them to calibrate verdict confidence. The confidence score is not a number derived from statistical confidence — it is derived from the match between the submission and the specific linguistic patterns he uses when genuinely excited versus when being polite. The confidence explanation tells you which tells were activated and what they mean.

7. FALSIFIABILITY (The Accuracy Layer)

This is what separates TasteGraph from a wrapper.

If you have built a taste model for someone, you can test it against things they have already publicly reacted to. For Paul Graham, we have a ground-truth dataset of documented reactions:

The Airbnb email exchange: January 23, 2009. Paul Graham wrote to Fred Wilson: "There's no reason this couldn't be as big as eBay. And this team is the right one to do it." Specific date, specific quote, published email, measurable outcome. Airbnb IPO'd at $75 billion. PG was right. Does the model predict he would have liked Airbnb in 2009? If not, the model is wrong.

The Reddit name controversy: PG told the founders their name was terrible and suggested "Octopop." He was wrong by $38 billion. Does the model predict he would dislike distinctive, playful consumer brand names? If so, the blind spot is correctly modeled.

The Dropbox solo founder rejection: Drew Houston was initially rejected for being a solo founder, reapplied with a cofounder, was accepted, built one of YC's biggest successes. Does the model capture PG's aversion to solo founders?

The Microsoft Is Dead essay: April 2007. "I can sense that. No one is even afraid of Microsoft anymore." Specific public prediction with specific reasoning and an 18-year outcome window.

Every taste profile includes a published accuracy score: "Estimated 78% accuracy — tested against 23 known public reactions." The methodology is visible. You can see which ground-truth cases were used, which the model predicted correctly, and which it missed. This is a falsifiable claim. A taste model with a published accuracy rate against known reactions is a research contribution, not a demo.

HOW THE RESEARCH ENGINE WORKS (NIA / NOZOMI)

TasteGraph uses Nozomi — Nia — as its autonomous research oracle. The key property that makes this work is depth and specificity. When building a taste profile, Nia does not run a single query and summarize the results. It runs a different research campaign for each taste dimension.

For the stated vs. actual dimension, it searches for documented public reactions — things the person has specifically praised or criticized — and separately searches for their stated principles. It then indexes both in parallel and creates the structural map of where they diverge.

For the blind spots dimension, it searches for cases where the person was publicly wrong, documented retrospectives where they acknowledged error, and cases where they succeeded despite their own skepticism. The Airbnb cereal box story, the Reddit name advice, the Dropbox rejection — these are not found by a single query. They require targeted searches for "PG wrong about," "YC rejected company that later succeeded," "Paul Graham reversed position on."

For the linguistic tells dimension, it retrieves the actual text of essays and interviews and runs corpus analysis: what words appear when he is expressing excitement, what hedges cluster around uncertainty, what rare language marks genuine strong disapproval.

The discovery phase surfaces sources as they are found: essays, interviews, podcast transcripts, controversy threads, investment letters, public posts. The indexing phase shows each source being processed. The aspect-building phase shows each dimension's research running in parallel. At no point is this a black box. You can see what the oracle found, what it indexed, and what it extracted.

After profile completion, profiles are cached. Subsequent judgments against the same person skip the profile phase and go directly to verdict generation. The cache means you can submit ten different things to Paul Graham without rebuilding the profile each time — the research is amortized.

HOW THE VERDICT ENGINE WORKS

After the profile is built, your submission is ingested. TasteGraph accepts text pasted directly, URLs (which Nia fetches and parses), GitHub repositories (which Nia reads), PDFs, and tweets. The submission is broken into content blocks. The ingestion event in the live feed shows you how many blocks were extracted: "Ingest — 8 content blocks ready."

Seven parallel judge agents launch, one per taste dimension. Each agent is seeded with:

- The full indexed profile for its dimension
- The submission content blocks
- Search access to retrieve additional evidence

Each agent reasons explicitly about the submission through its dimension's lens. The stated vs. actual agent looks for tensions between what the submission claims and what the person's documented behavior suggests. The blind spots agent looks for overlap with the person's documented error categories. The linguistic tells agent looks for elements of the submission that would activate the person's specific excitement or disapproval signals.

The agents' reasoning is streamed live to the feed. You can see them thinking. You can see what they search for. You can see them find things. Each agent completes independently; the spider chart spoke for that dimension goes solid when it finishes.

After all agents complete, a synthesis pass takes all seven sub-verdicts and produces the final output structure. The synthesis is not an average — it is a weighted combination that accounts for temporal weighting, blind spot corrections, and context adjustments.

The final verdict arrives as a complete structured object:

- summary: the overall assessment in the person's voice
- score: a number out of ten
- confidence_score: calibrated by linguistic tells
- confidence_explanation: which tells were activated and what they indicate
- loves: a list of items the person would praise, each with aspect, because, confidence, and evidence citations
- hates: a list of items they would criticize, same structure
- would_change: specific suggestions, with evidence
- contradiction_callout: the gap between stated values and documented behavior, applied to this verdict
- blind_spot_warning: which error categories apply, with correction suggestion
- influence_lineage_note: which parts of their intellectual lineage are most relevant here
- temporal_weight_note: how the time-weighting affected the score
- context_variance: the applied context score delta plus alternative contexts and their deltas
- falsifiability: the accuracy estimate and methodology

Every field in the output is grounded. Citations are traceable. The score has a derivation. The confidence has an explanation. Nothing is generated without a basis.

The evidence pills are the most load-bearing component. Every inference in the verdict is backed by specific evidence. The NuanceBanners for contradiction and blind spot callouts are visually distinct from the rest of the verdict. WHY WE CARE ABOUT LLM TASTE

Language models have taste. This is not metaphorical. The patterns in what a model generates — what it finds elegant, what it elides, what it emphasizes, what it considers a well-formed argument versus a weak one — reflect the aesthetic preferences latent in its training. When you ask a model to evaluate something, you are getting a verdict shaped by that latent taste, which is usually invisible, ungrounded, and impossible to audit.

We think this is a problem worth solving for several reasons.

First: the models that will matter most are the ones with good taste — taste that matches what the best builders and thinkers actually care about. Right now, most models have averaged-out taste. They generate what is statistically normal, not what is actually good. The difference between a model that can recognize a genuinely novel idea and one that cannot is a difference in taste, not a difference in capability. Building systems that encode specific, well-documented taste profiles is a path toward evaluation quality that goes beyond generic helpfulness.

Second: taste is falsifiable. This is the key claim. If you say a model has Paul Graham's taste, you can test it. Did it predict correctly that he would praise Airbnb in 2009? Did it predict he would dislike the Reddit name? Did it capture his blind spot about consumer brand judgment? If not, the taste model is wrong and you can improve it. Most AI evaluation — whether a model is "helpful," "accurate," "appropriate" — is not falsifiable in this precise way. TasteGraph is. A taste model that can be tested against known historical reactions is a research contribution, not just a product.

Third: the hard problem is not stated values — it is actual reactions. LLMs can already summarize what someone has written about their values. GPT-4 can tell you what Paul Graham says he cares about. The hard problem is reconstructing how he would respond to something new, given not just what he says he cares about but the full pattern of how he has actually reacted in the past — including the contradictions, the drift, the blind spots, the tells. That is a modeling problem, not a retrieval problem. TasteGraph is our approach to that modeling problem.

THE TECHNICAL ARCHITECTURE

Backend: Node.js server with a single /api/judge endpoint that delivers a streaming SSE response. The agent loop runs against the OpenAI API. Profile building uses Nozomi for autonomous research and structured extraction. Profiles are stored as a directory per person with one file per dimension.

Profile structure:
- profile.json: core structured profile data
- stated_vs_actual.md: the contradiction layer — stated principles indexed separately from documented reactions, with citations
- taste_drift.md: temporal model of how the person's expressed values have shifted, with timeline
- blind_spots.md: documented error categories with specific cases and accuracy analysis
- influence_graph.md: the intellectual lineage — who shaped them, what that means for evaluation weighting
- contextual_variation.md: how judgment shifts by context — stakes, framing, audience
- linguistic_tells.md: excitement signals, uncertainty patterns, disapproval markers, with corpus analysis
- falsifiability.md: the ground-truth dataset — documented public reactions with dates, quotes, outcomes

Frontend: React with custom SSE streaming via the Fetch API and a ReadableStream reader. Three stages: input, research, verdict. Spider chart visualization built as a custom SVG component. Evidence pills, confidence bars, nuance banners, context chips — each is a custom component built to render a specific kind of structured data from the verdict object.

The streaming architecture means the UI is never waiting. Every event from the backend arrives in real time. The research phase can take several minutes; the entire design of the research stage — the live feed, the spider chart, the labeled events — is built around making those minutes feel informative rather than dead. You are not watching a spinner. You are watching the oracle work.

THE META-POINT

TasteGraph is submitting itself to TasteGraph. This document is a submission to the Paul Graham profile. The system will now judge, using Paul Graham's indexed taste across all seven dimensions, whether he would like TasteGraph itself.

This is a useful test. Paul Graham cares deeply about whether something is solving a real problem for real users. He cares whether the founders understand the domain they are operating in. He has strong views about what constitutes a research contribution versus a demo, and documented skepticism about AI products that wrap an existing model without adding fundamental value.

He also has documented blind spots that are relevant here: he has historically underestimated the value of taste infrastructure and aesthetic modeling. He has been ambivalent about developer tools versus consumer products. He sometimes misses the research contribution in systems that look like products. The blind spot warning card in the verdict TasteGraph produces on itself should account for this.

The contradiction callout should surface that he says he values pure merit but his actual evaluation would be heavily influenced by whether this feels like "the kind of thing a determined hacker would build" — which is a culturally-specific signal, not a neutral one.

The linguistic tells analysis should look for whether the elements of the submission activate his genuine excitement patterns — the "most exciting kind of idea: one that seems both preposterous and true" construction — or whether it would get polite hedged approval.

The verdict TasteGraph produces on itself will test whether it has actually modeled these things correctly. If it produces a generic positive verdict without engaging the contradiction layer and the blind spot warning, it has failed. If it surfaces the real tensions — that PG would find the falsifiability framing compelling but would be uncertain about market size and would worry about whether this is a wrapper — then the model is working.

WHY THIS IS NOT JUST A WRAPPER

The fundamental challenge is not building another chatbot that summarizes what someone has said. That is solved. Every LLM can do that. The challenge is:

1. Modeling the gap between stated values and actual reactions — requires separately indexing stated principles and documented reactions, then mapping the divergence structurally
2. Temporal weighting of taste drift — requires timeline-aware synthesis that weights recent signals differently from old ones, not just an average across everything
3. Blind spot calibration — requires documented error analysis, not just positive signals; requires knowing where the person has been wrong and by how much
4. Influence graph construction — requires mapping the intellectual lineage and understanding how it weights different parts of the evaluation
5. Linguistic tell indexing — requires corpus analysis across a person's full published output, not just knowing their topics
6. Falsifiable accuracy scoring — requires building a ground-truth dataset of known reactions, running predictions against it, and publishing the accuracy with methodology

None of these are retrieval. All of them are modeling. TasteGraph does all six, in parallel, with visible research, in a streaming UI that makes the depth legible at every step. You can see the oracle discover sources. You can see the profile being built spoke by spoke. You can see the agents reason. You can see every citation. You can expand the methodology and see the derivation of every number.

That is the contribution: not that we can tell you what Paul Graham thinks, but that we can show you how we know, let you test whether we are right, and give you the full topology of his judgment — including the parts of it he does not know about himself.
